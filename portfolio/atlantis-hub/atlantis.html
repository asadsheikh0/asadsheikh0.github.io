atlantis hub

data-driven display

interaction design studio i, fall 2019

team members: asad sheikh, jai sawkar, jialu li

overview

This project in Interaction Design Studio I was about creating a data-driven display for Atlantis Hub, a fictitious travel station located in the San Juan islands. As a group, we would have to determine how to best organize a large set of data as well as account for the different types of users that might interact with them.

My role in the group revolved around organizing the data in terms of how to group data as well as what important details we needed to include.

persona analysis

We were given the personas of 3 individuals who would interact with the display in 3 different ways. We first went through each persona and determined what they valued in their travel experience and how we could include that in a design.

As we analyzed each persona, we looked at the stakeholder's needs and goals and how that translated into the design. We decided that the design of our dashboard should advertise the PTF pass, which was the stakeholder's new creation to encourage travel among the San Juan islands.

For both of the users, we looked at their needs in a travel experience as well as potential use cases for the display for their travel needs. For each of the users, we then plotted their travel preferences for both mode of transport as well as how efficient they wanted their travel experience to be.

data analysis

The next step in the process was to look at the data that had been given to us to present. We were essentially given a very large spreadsheet and had to filter out information that we believed was not needed for the display.

We chose to organize all travel data first by location, and then by mode, consistent with what already exists in terms of travel dashboards such as flight status monitors. This way, when the users first approach the display, they would first find their destination and then see it grouped by mode of transport.

data + personas

In addition to analyzing the data given to us, we also analyzed the needs of the personas to graph where they stand on a concept map of speed vs. flexibility. From there, we identified what each of the personas would need to see in a display, and also determined what information did not need to be included in the final display.

initial iteration

After consolidating our data, we knew we wanted to sort our data Destination first. Our initial, low-fidelity model illustrates just that, with a map for context center-focused on the display.

digital iteration

Once we had the ground work for our initial display, we created a digital version to help us understand not only the content, but how the content reads.

We presented this to our peers, and understood that though this was a good start, we still had a way to go. Some of the feedback included that there were not enough options listed; moreover, not all of the travel destinations were listed at the same time, making a user have to wait for the display rotation to restart.
