bun bun bakery

functional web site design

programming usable interfaces, spring 2020

overview

This project in Programming Usable Interfaces was about creating a website for a business that encouraged user interaction. I chose to create a website for Bun Bun Bake Shop, a fictitious local bakery in Pittsburgh that sold delicious cinnamon rolls with a variety of flavors and glazings.

paper prototyping

At the start of this project, in class we were first tasked with coming up with low-fidelity paper prototypes which we then shared with classmates.

As a part of my first design, I decided to use a grid-based layout to display the variety of cinnamon roll flavors. As I user-tested this prototype, clicks on a particular flavor would then direct the user to choose more details about their order - choosing their preferred glazing and quantity, and then adding their order to the cart.

After my first round of testing, I received feedback to include a back button in case the user makes a mistake and needs to go back and to keep the continuity of the bar menu at the top of the page so that the user could easily navigate through the site, regardless of what page they were on. These changes are reflected in the second iteration, shown to the right of the first iteration.

continuing iterations

After the first iteration of changes, we then had to test again on our revisions. Meeting up with another classmate, I then had them perform the same task I had my first tester perform but with my modified prototype. After the test, I got some more valuable feedback which I then incorporated into my next iteration.

This time, I received feedback saying that I should include a semantic indicator of what the arrow at the bottom of the first page meant - scroll. Another thing I received feedback on was that it wasn't clear if a user could add multiple of one flavor but with different quanities or glazings.

After hearing this feedback, I then included an intermediate order page that would display the current item the user was planning to add on their cart for each flavor. That way, a user would be able to add multiple items to their cart of the same flavor but with various quantities and glazings.

increasing fidelity

After iterating with a few paper prototypes, I then began to develop a higher-fidelity prototype of the website. I worked in Figma to create a site map of all the different pages that I wanted to include as well as including the user flow between pages.

In terms of my final design decisions, I decided to first allow the users to navigate to a products page where they can pick a flavor that they want to buy as that is the primary grouping principle for ordering these cinnamon rolls. Once they select a flavor, they can then select a quantity and a glazing and add it to their order. The order does not add it straight to the cart so that users can order multiple of the same flavor but with different glaze without being sent back to the flavor selection screen. Thus, once the user finished coming up with orders of a flavor, they then confirm their selections and add the intermediate order to the cart. Once they navigate to the cart, they can then choose their shipping method, either to their home address or pick it up in the store. After that, they can then input their payment details before pressing submit and then receiving their wonderful and delicious cinnamon rolls.

adding functionality

After creating a higher-fidelity prototype, we then began the process of translating our designs into HTML and CSS. This part of the process was particularly difficult for me, as I had no prior experience with either HTML or CSS, so it took a lot of time to get working.

Additionally, we were not allowed to use any frameworks such as Bootstrap, so getting everything to align together was a lot of work, but it all worked out in the end!
